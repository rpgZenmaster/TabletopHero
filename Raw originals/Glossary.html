The Normal Project Glossary.html


Please note that many of the Glossary.html entries are ripped from or at least inspired by the entries from "The Provisional Glossary.html" by Ron Edwards, at The Forge. Entries that are direct quotes of the entries given on The Forge are indicated by a Courier New font-face. In some cases, definitions include lingo that isn't defined in this Glossary.html. I decided not to keep such lingo since it starts to lead outside of what I'm interested in for our game. Furthermore, Mr. Edward's Glossary was written from a perspective recognizing only three Creative Agendas, while Mr. Bartle's models recognize up to eight player types. These classification systems are not necessarily incompatible, but come at the subject angle from different angles. See "Theory Development" and "Current Work" for more information.

Also see "I Have No Words & I Must Design: Toward a Critical Vocabulary for Games" by Greg Costikyan.



achiever - One of the four player types that results when the implicit/explicit axis of Richard Bartle's 3D Player Types model is collapsed. (See the "Virtual Worlds: Why People Play" Introduction by Richard Bartle.) Achievers are in the World/Active quadrant, and therefore feel most rewarded when their accomplishments are validated within the structure and rules of the game universe itself. In other words, they prefer to win games in the most traditional sense of the word "win". Compare to explorer, killer, and socializer.



Bartle Ladder, the - My own term for the roughly ladder-like structure that results when the most common paths followed by players are overlapped onto Richard Bartle's 3D Player Types model. (See the article "Virtual Worlds: Why People Play" Introduction by Richard Bartle.)



Bartle Path, the - My term for the four-point path that results when the most common paths followed by players are overlapped onto Richard Bartle's 3D Player Types model, as for the Bartle Ladder, and then the model is collapsed along the Players/World axis. (See the "Virtual Worlds: Why People Play" Introduction by Richard Bartle.) Descriptions of each of the terms "seeker", "learner", "doer", and "master" are found elsewhere in this Glossary.html.



Big Model, the - Built by Ron Edwards, the Big Model suggests that the layers of a particular instance of a role playing game are rather like an onion, with each layer being encapsulated within another. The four recognized layers are: Social Contract (outermost), Exploration, Techniques, and Ephemera (innermost). The model also recognizes three modes of play, called Creative Agendas, which are: Gamism, Narrativism, Simulationism. The four layers are treated as the medium, while the Creative Agenda is the ultimate point of playing, that the players want to express using that medium. I believe the example given on The Forge is of a painter, though I can no longer find it. The choice of what the subject should be for the painting is the painter's Creative Agenda. The colours and brushes and canvas he chooses are his medium. Some choices of medium are better than others for expressing any given subject.




Also see http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Big_Model



combat wheel - See the entry for Normal Device.



creative agenda - The aesthetic priorities and any matters of imaginative interest regarding role-playing. Three distinct Creative Agendas are currently recognized: Step On Up (Gamist), The Right to Dream (Simulationist), and Story Now (Narrativist). <...> Creative Agenda is expressed using all Components of Exploration, but most especially System.



doer - From the Bartle Path, the category of players called "doers" describes those have discovered what skills are needed to play (or at least, those skills needed to play in a particular way that they want) and are good at using those skills. They are described as having conscious competence in those skills, per the article http://www.businessballs.com/consciouscompetencelearningmodel.htm.



ephemera - The moment-to-moment events that take place during a roleplaying game; e.g. the dice rolled, tables checked, and sentences spoken. Any given technique consists of a string of one or more ephemera.
epic simulationism - See simulationism, epic.



exploration - Distinct from the player type "explorer", all participation in role playing games ultimately involves a certain amount of exploration, usually of the shared imagined space. (The system can also be explored.) It is collectively created and shared among the players and game master. It comprises the meat of what people typically mean when they talk about traditional role-playing. There is emphasis on the fact that the shared imagined space is created collectively, with input from all players. Without this layer, the activity might more aptly be called story-telling or fiction-building.



explorer - Distinct from "exploration", the explorer is one of the four player types that results when the implicit/explicit axis of Richard Bartle's 3D Player Types model is collapsed. (See the "Virtual Worlds: Why People Play" Introduction by Richard Bartle.) Explorers are in the World/Interactive quadrant, and therefore feel most rewarded when learning about and mastering the game universe, in the sense of becoming "good at" the game, even though the actual played character may not be powerful. Compare to achiever, killer, and socializer.



friend - As a player type, friends' avatars exist in the virtual or imagined world, but players who fall into this category focus more on interacting with the other players behind the avatars. On the 3D Player Types model presented in Richard Bartle's article "Virtual Worlds: Why People Play" Introduction, friends are in the Implicit, Players, Interacting octant.



Fruitful Void, the - A term originally coined by Vincent Baker of http://www.lumpley.com, the Fruitful Void is the collection of emergent attributes of a tabletop RPG. The Fruitful Void defines what a given tabletop game is "about" in the most meaningful sense. In a similar way, Shakespeare's "Romeo and Juliet" is involves two particular lovers and their quarrelling families but is about the turmoil felt by the lovers having to choose between each other and their senses of duty to their respective families. In a tabletop RPG, this Fruitful Void may not correlate with the game designers' intended experience depending on how well they build the game. For example, a game designer may base a game upon Romeo and Juliet with the intention of evoking the same emotional turmoil in the players (i.e. a Narrativist CA), but if the Romeo character scores "experience points" for killing Capulets, it may result in a purely Gamist game.



game master or GM - The game master basically runs the physics of the shared-imagined universe and controls all the non-player characters. In many cases, the GM also acts as a game referee, deciding whether or not a PC's actions violated any rules.



game mechanic - Individual and specific features of System; Mechanics in text form are "rules".



gamist, gamism - Gamism is one of the three major modes of play (i.e. Creative Agendas) recognized in the Big Model. Although immersion in the fantasy world is important, the primary focus of this mode is on the competition between real players of the game. See "Gamism: Step On Up" by Ron Edwards.



griefer - Griefers act on other players with no sharp, specific goals in mind beyond the intention usually to hurt or help. This is the beginning state of players who are new to the game but prefer to focus on the other participants in the game rather than the virtual or imagined world. On the 3D Player Types model presented in Richard Bartle's article "Virtual Worlds: Why People Play" Introduction, griefers are in the Implicit, Players, Acting octant.



hacker - As a player type, hackers are more interested in playing with the boundaries and rules of the virtual or imagined game world than anything else. On the 3D Player Types model presented in Richard Bartle's article "Virtual Worlds: Why People Play" Introduction, hackers are in the Implicit, World, Interacting octant.



handling time - The real time required to process, calculate, and interpret a resolution Technique once its procedures have been applied. See also Search Time.



Hero's Journey, the - The predictable pattern that most players will follow when they encounter a new fictional environment through an avatar. The pattern is: Seeker, Learner, Doer, Master. See Richard Bartle's textbook "Designing Virtual Worlds" for more information. The term was originally coined by Joseph Campbell in his 1949 book "The Hero With A Thousand Faces".



house rules - See rules.



immersion - From "Virtual Worlds: Why People Play" Introduction by Richard Bartle: "Immersion is the sense that a player has of being in a virtual world. It is related to the concept of presence – the illusion that a (computer-)mediated experience is not mediated [Lombard97]. Immersion is also related to the psychological concept of flow – a deep involvement that transcends distractions and sense of time [Csikszentmihalyi90]."



incoherence - Play which includes incompatible combinations of Creative Agendas among participants. Incoherent play is considered to contribute to Dysfunctional play, but does not define it. Incoherence may be applied indirectly to game rules. Abashedness represents a minor, correctable form of Incoherence.



killer - One of the four player types that results when the implicit/explicit axis of Richard Bartle's 3D Player Types model is collapsed. (See the "Virtual Worlds: Why People Play" Introduction by Richard Bartle.) Killers are in the Player/Active quadrant, and therefore feel most rewarded by acting upon and eliciting a response from other players. Although the connotation of "killer" is negative, this player type includes those that enjoy teaching and aiding other players, for no other reason than the good feeling they get from doing so. It can also include, say, politicians, who enjoy exerting power over other people though not necessarily in a violent way. Compare to achiever, explorer, and socializer.



learner - From the Bartle Path, a learner has become familiar with the game that they have chosen a "career path". In many MUDs, the players have the freedom to choose what skills they would like to learn, and at this stage, the player is aware he/she needs to learn them. The player is said to have conscious incompetence in the skills, as discussed in http://www.businessballs.com/consciouscompetencelearningmodel.htm.



Lumpley Principle, the - "System (including but not limited to 'the rules') is defined as the means by which the group agrees to imagined events during play." The author of the principle is Vincent Baker, see Vincent's standard rant: power, credibility, and assent and Player power abuse.



maker - From the Bartle Path, players in this category have successfully gained a set of skills they can use to play the game in question, though they still need to actively concentrate in order to use them. Such players are said to have conscious competence in these skills, per the article http://www.businessballs.com/consciouscompetencelearningmodel.htm.



master - From the Bartle Path, a player in the master category is so highly skilled at some set of methods of play that they come naturally now. Using these skills requires no conscious effort, and so the player is said to have unconscious competence in them, as discussed in http://www.businessballs.com/consciouscompetencelearningmodel.htm.



mechanic - See "game mechanic"



MUD or multi-user dungeon - Alternatively, MOG (multiplayer online game), an online electronic game where multiple people can interact in a single online environment, usually for the purposes of playing a game (in which case, the people are called "players") and usually with each person controlling a single avatar. The MUD acronym is an ancestor for the modern acronym MMORPG (massively-multiplayer online role playing game). Also see http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MUD.



narrativist, narrativism - Narrativism is one of the three major modes of play (i.e. Creative Agendas) recognized in the Big Model. Although immersion in the fantasy world is important, the primary focus of this mode is on the resolution of the Premise (where Premise is a loaded term here). See "Narrativism: Story Now" by Ron Edwards.



networker - Networks prefer to interact with people and enjoy engaging in social circles. On the 3D Player Types model presented in Richard Bartle's article "Virtual Worlds: Why People Play" Introduction, networkers are in the Explicit, Players, Interacting octant.



non-player character or NPC - Virtually all non-player characters are background characters, though some play a significant role in the game. A non-player character is controlled by the game master (and, rarely, sometimes purely by game mechanics).



Normal Device - Also called the "Combat Wheel", it is the central action-resolution mechanic for the Normal Project. Its workings shall remain a mystery for now. Note that although the Normal Device will be the central action-resolution mechanic, the ultimate game should have a few facets so that it may fill the niche for which the game is intended.



Normal Project - The project for which this entire Wiki is devoted. Visit home for more details. In brief, the goal of the Normal Project is to build an exciting RPG that appeals to many peoples' tastes and which makes good use of the Normal Device.



opportunist - Opportunists prefer to play with the virtual or imagined environment, but they have no particular goal in mind. This the state of players who prefer participating with the virtual or imagined world, but who are still new to it. On the 3D Player Types model presented in Richard Bartle's article "Virtual Worlds: Why People Play" Introduction, opportunists are in the Implicit, World, Acting octant.



planner - Planners are those players who enjoy using the skills they have learned and knowledge they have gained about the environment (virtual or imagined) to bring about their own desired results. On the 3D Player Types model presented in Richard Bartle's article "Virtual Worlds: Why People Play" Introduction, planners are in the Explicit, World, Acting octant.



player character or PC - A character in an RPG controlled by a player who is not the game master.



politician - Players who enjoy exerting the influence they have gained in the virtual or imagined environment on other players. On the 3D Player Types model presented in Richard Bartle's article "Virtual Worlds: Why People Play" Introduction, politicians are in the Explicit, Players, Acting octant. Though I haven't tested this claim, I expect this includes non-player characters in shared imagined worlds due to the fact that all NPCs in this scenario are still ultimately controlled by a human being, namely the GM. This is not true in virtual worlds.



premise - (adapted from Egri[3]) A generalizable, problematic aspect of human interactions. Early in the process of creating or experiencing a story, a Premise is best understood as a proposition or perhaps an ideological challenge to the world represented by the protagonist's passions. Later in the process, resolving the conflicts of the story transforms Premise into a theme - a judgmental statement about how to act, behave, or believe. In role-playing, "protagonist" typically indicates a character mainly controlled by one person. A defining feature of Story Now.



reward - The word reward in its usual context refers strictly to the benefits and awards given to the PCs upon achieving some victory. Less often, it refers to the personal and social gratification experienced by a player due to events unfolding in the RPG. A given reward need not also be a reward for the character, nor need it even be within the SIS. An example of this could be the hideous painful death of a player's character, which the player finds entertaining for reasons of drama. Also see reward cycle.



reward cycle - The reward cycle is the explicit or emergent cycle through which the PCs go to achieve "rewards", in the sense of the personal and social gratification as experienced by the player. A single game need not have just one reward cycle. An emergent reward cycle may circumvent a game designer's intended reward cycle if the game is designed incoherently.



roleplaying game - The definition of what constitutes a roleplaying game is difficult to narrow down, but in essence it is a game consisting of the exploration of a SIS. Two good resources are the article "Hearts, Clubs, Diamonds, Spades: Players Who Suit MUDs" by Mr. Richard Bartle and the Big Model, built with tremendous input from Ron Edwards. The former was written specifically about MUDs, but the same arguments may apply to roleplaying games to some degree.


rules - Rules refers to the set of instructions given for any particular game or RPG. Such instructions may exclude subsets of the instructions given by the game designer(s) and/or include extra instructions created by a particular set of players. The aforementioned extra instructions are oftentimes called "house rules".



scientist - A type of player who, as the category name suggests, enjoys testing the rules and boundaries of a world (virtual, imagined, or otherwise). On the 3D Player Types model presented in Richard Bartle's article "Virtual Worlds: Why People Play" Introduction, scientists are in the Explicit, World, Interacting octant.



screentime - Screentime is the time spent by a PC in the spotlight. In essence, it is the amount of time that all other players take a step back and allow a PC to perform actions.



search time - The real time required to determine necessary values or information prior to applying a resolution Technique. See also Handling Time.



seeker - From the Bartle Path, a seeker is a type of player who is ignorant of the methods of play, and isn't even aware of what those methods are. Typically, such a player is either new to the game or hasn't yet learned how to play using a specific subset of the rules. A seeker displays "unconscious incompetence" as discussed in http://www.businessballs.com/consciouscompetencelearningmodel.htm.



shared imagined space or SIS - The imaginary environment collectively built by the players and the game master through usage of System.



simulationist, simulationism - Simulationism is one of the three major modes of play (i.e. Creative Agendas) recognized in the Big Model. The primary focus of this mode is to achieve immersion within the fantasy itself as a priority over other goals, such as interpersonal agendas between the players. See "Simulationism: The Right to Dream" by Ron Edwards.



simulationism, epic - Epic Simulationism is a subset of Simulationism where exploration is simply a means to an end, that end being the player's exploration of his or her own identity.



spotlight - The proverbial spotlight is a reference to the attention given to a player by the other players of a tabletop RPG, while that player's character is active. This term is virtually never used in reference to the game master, because the game master typically doesn't have a PC and therefore is never truly "onscreen" (see screentime).



social contract - All interactions and relationships among the role-playing group, including emotional connections, logistic arrangements, and expectations. All role-playing is a subset of the Social Contract.



socializer - One of the four player types that results when the implicit/explicit axis of Richard Bartle's 3D Player Types model is collapsed. (See the "Virtual Worlds: Why People Play" Introduction by Richard Bartle.) Socializers are in the Social/Interactive quadrant, and therefore feel most rewarded by interacting with other human beings on an emotional level, even if engaging in nothing more than simple conversation. Compare to achiever, explorer, and killer.



stance - The positioning of the player's perspective and mode of control, relative to that player's character.
  • Actor: The person playing a character determines the character's decisions and actions using only knowledge and perceptions that the character would have. This stance does not necessarily include identifying with the character and feeling what he or she "feels," nor does it require in-character dialogue.
  • Author: The person playing a character determines the character's decisions and actions based on the person's priorities, independently of the character's knowledge and perceptions. Author Stance may or may not include a retroactive "motivation" of the character to perform the actions. When it lacks this feature, it is called Pawn Stance.
  • Director: The person playing a character determines aspects of the environment relative to the character in some fashion, entirely separately from the character's knowledge or ability to influence events. Therefore the player has not only determined the character's actions, but the context, timing, and spatial circumstances of those actions, or even features of the world separate from the characters. Director Stance is often confused with narration of an in-game event, but the two concepts are not necessarily related.
  • Pawn: A subset of Author Stance which lacks the retroactive "motivation" of the character to perform the actions. Often but wrongly identified with Gamist play.



statistic - From http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistic_(role-playing_games), "A statistic (or stat) in role-playing games is a piece of data which represents a particular aspect of a fictional character. That piece of data is usually a (unitless) integer or, in some cases, a set of dice."



system - System consists of the whole collection of Techniques which make up the game for a given instance of roleplaying. Effectively "the game". See also the Lumpley Principle.



technique - Techniques are the agreed upon structures through which events forward in the game and usually also in the shared imagined space. An example of a technique which moves the game forward but which does not move events forward in the SIS is the refreshing of a "Luck" statistic as a consequence of certain conditions and which has no reality within the SIS but may affect it later. Each technique consists of ephemera.



table-top hero model - Also see 'epic simulationism', a type of simulationism where what's most important to simulate is an epic pattern of adventure, call the Hero's Journey, or Monomyth.
