Roleplaying Theory -


  • The Forge: http://www.indie-rpgs.com - A forum for discussing RPG theory. The main moderator is Ron Edwards, who has spent a lot of time studying the roleplaying gameindustry. An enormous amount of insight can be drawn from the website by studying the theory texts.
    • http://indie-rpgs.com/_articles/glossary.html a.k.a. The Forge's Provisional Glossary - The glossary which provided the inspiration for the glossary on this wiki. However, it tends to be very jargon-heavy, and is difficult to understand unless you're familiar with the Big Model.
    • http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Big_Model - To quote Wikipedia, the Big Model is a body of role-playing game theory developed primarily by Ron Edwards. It serves as a capstone and organizing principle to the amorphous body of work commonly referred to as GNS Theory.
    • "GNS and Other Matters of Role-playing Theory" by Ron Edwards - Basically, a foundational essay for the Big Model.
    • "Simulationism: The Right to Dream" by Ron Edwards - An essay expounding upon the simulationist mode of play. Briefly, the goal of simulationism is to have the immersion of oneself in the fiction of the game world take priority over any interpersonal goals that may exist or arise between the players.
    • "Gamism: Step On Up" by Ron Edwards - An essay expounding upon the gamist mode of play. Briefly, gamism focuses upon healthy competition between the players as opposed to cooperation against the challenges of the game world.
    • "Narrativism: Story Now" by Ron Edwards - An essay expounding upon the narrativist mode of play. Briefly, narrativism focuses upon the resolution of Premise through play within the game world, where Premise is a loaded term; i.e. please see the Glossary.
    • "Silent Railroading and the Intersection of Scenario Prep & Player Authorship" - A Forge post containing Ron Edwards' take on the GM.
    • "(High Fantasy) First Thoughts for a new system" - A Forge post containing an idea by Ron Edwards' for tying character effort to output. Good for varied resource management (per Brian Gleichman's "Elements of Tactics").
    • "Ruminations on the Impossible Dream Before Breakfast" - The Forge post where I drop the notion of attempting to incorporate Narrativism in my game designs.

  • Classic RPG Realms: The Answer to the Edition Wars: The Big Model - A decent exposition to Ron Edward's Big Model, for those of the feint of heart who aren't willing to jump into the Forge yet.

  • http://www.rpg.net - As far as I can tell, a much more widely-read forum than the Forge. Though the posts written by the various users can occasionally be interesting, more often than not they veer off into the usual aimless internet babbling.
    • http://www.rpg.net/columneditor/mendespinto/RQ08_t1.pdf "Primary Attributes in RPGs 2005" by Sergio Mascarenhas - A survey of a vast array of different RPGs and their primary character attributes.

  • http://www.lumpley.com/comment.php?entry=119 "The Fruitful Void" by Vincent Baker from Lumpley Games - An article about what's really most important about RPGs but hardest to understand and see. It's what the game is really about.

  • http://www.lulu.com/product/file-download/quick-primer-for-old-school-gaming/3159558 "A Quick Primer for Old School Gaming" by Matthew Finch - Supposedly a primer to get back to the heyday of D&D when gaming when fun and exciting, and didn't include all the little chores.

  • http://www.madbrewlabs.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/08/rpg-design-patterns-9-26-05.pdf "Design Patterns of Successful RPGs" by Whitson John Kirk III - An interesting survey of the internal mechanics of existing popular RPGs, reducing them to an engineer's perspective.


Virtual Environment Theory -


  • http://www.mud.co.uk/richard/VWWPP.pdf"Virtual Worlds: Why People Play" Introduction - A fairly expansive introduction to the full document presented in Richard Bartle's book, discussing the various player types in regards to electronic virtual worlds, and peoples' progressions through those types. Their progression is described as a Hero's Journey.
    • The pre-cursor to this article, http://www.mud.co.uk/richard/hcds.htm "Hearts, Clubs, Diamonds, Spades: Players Who Suit MUDs", again by Richard Bartle.
    • http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Designing_Virtual_Worlds "Designing Virtual Worlds" by Richard Bartle - The textbook of which the article is a part.
    • http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Hero_With_A_Thousand_Faces - Wikipedia entry for "The Hero With a Thousand Faces" by Joseph Campbell, first published in 1949. Richard Bartle's work depends heavily upon the ideas presented in this book.

  • http://www.escapistmagazine.com/articles/view/issues/issue_30/182-In-Celebration-of-the-Inner-Rogue "In Celebration of the Inner Rogue" by Mark Wallace - An anecdote describing Richard Bartle's "Hero's Journey" in action. (See above entry.)

  • http://depts.washington.edu/edtech/wittmer.pdf "Measuring Presence in Virtual Environments: A Presence Questionnaire" by Bob G. Witmer and Michael J. Singer - A questionnaire apparently used to gather data on peoples' feelings of "presence" within a virtual environment. This link is to the description of the study and questionnaire, but does not include actual data from tests.


General Game Theory -


  • http://www.rpg.net/news+reviews/columns/elements01nov02.html "Elements of Tactics" by Brian Gleichman - A brilliant article currently residing on RPG.net that discusses what makes for a really juicy, tactical game. Hint: it ain't just a matter of building a rock, paper, scissors game.

  • http://www.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtgcom/daily/mr85 "The Value of Pie" by Mark Rosewater - Although the article is specifically about the color pie of the Magic: The Gathering game, the concepts discussed are similar to those discussed in Brian Gleichman's "Elements of Tactics" and therefore apply to good strategy games in general.

  • Gilbert's Rules of Thumb by Ron Gilbert (edited by Daniel Blain) adapted from http://grumpygamer.com/2152210 - An article regarding puzzle-building. Although the article is regarding single-player adventure games, the same arguments apply directly to tabletop and electronic RPGs.

  • http://www.costik.com/nowords2002.pdf "I Have No Words & I Must Design: Toward a Critical Vocabulary for Games" by Greg Costikyan - An analysis of what it means to play a game and how to classify games, or whether classification is even possible.

  • http://www.businessballs.com/consciouscompetencelearningmodel.htm "conscious competence learning model", unknown author - An analysis of stages of learning. More general in nature than Richard Bartle's research and yet the resultant models and conclusions are much the same.


LARP Game Theory -

  • http://knutepunkt.laiv.org/kp05/Incentives%20as%20tools%20of%20larp%20dramaturgy..pdf "Incentives as tools of larp dramaturgy" by Unknown - From the Summary: provides an overview and analysis of "incentive", methods employed larpwrights to encourage specific player interactions during the larp - conflict, puzzles, fates, etc. and how they can be structured into connected "webs".

  • Shape_of_the_US_LARP_Hobby3.pdf by Amber Eagar - A discussion of the nature of LARPs Although the document itself doesn't reference tabletop RPGs much, it is enlightening to consider. A copy of the document is kept on the internet but it no longer seems to work in my copy of Acrobat Reader.
